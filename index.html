Public squares are often the site of historical events and continuous interaction between members of a community or institution. At the University of Chicago, the place that best fulfills this mission is the Main Quadrangles, which form not only the physical center of campus but also the center of the many communities that make up the university as a whole.

Scroll Down

Here, different UChicago groups that do not otherwise interact elsewhere cross paths with each other: undergraduate students (the College), post-graduate students, faculty, administrators, and others. The quadrangles have been an emblematic feature of the university campus since its initial conception, having been imagined as the focal point by lead architect Henry Ives Cobb before a single cornerstone was even laid on campus in the 1890s.
The Quadrangles of the University of Chicago as a Public Square
Written by Alan Cano
Design and Development by Austin Steinhart for the Chicago Maroon

This project will look at the original intent and design of the quadrangles, and how their design has encouraged social interaction and events at the university, from 1890 to 2025.

The following narration is not an exhaustive history; rather, it traces the contours of shifting values and priorities that have contributed to the University’s current institutional identity.

▸ Read about our Methodology

This project drew data from several sources, including official University publications, campus maps, municipal records, satellite imagery, the University of Chicago Map Collection, and news articles. The Maroon requested a manipulable version of the campus maps which the University did not provide. Most building footprints were obtained from OpenStreetMap, a crowdsourced geospatial data repository; some historic building footprints were recreated from historic maps using Felt. Many dates of building ownership were sourced from the University of Chicago Heritage Map, opening announcements, and University press releases. Maps are sourced from the University of Chicago Map Collection, the University of Chicago Photographic Archive, and include Racial Restrictive Covenants on Chicago's South Side in 1947.
